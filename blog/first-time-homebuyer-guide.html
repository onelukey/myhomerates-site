The Complete First-Time Homebuyer Checklist

This is a condensed, practical checklist version of our complete first-time homebuyer guide. Use it as a quick reference as you move through each stage of the process — print it, save it, or just work through it section by section.

Who this guide is for

First-time buyers who want a practical action checklist rather than a long explainer. For the full detail behind each item, see our complete first-time buyer guide.

1. Financial preparation checklist

Before you start house hunting
Pull your free credit reports at AnnualCreditReport.com and dispute any errors
Calculate a realistic personal budget — not just your maximum lender approval
Save your down payment plus an additional 2-5% for closing costs
Build a separate emergency fund — don't spend your entire savings on the purchase
Avoid new debt, credit applications, or major purchases
Gather documentation — pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, ID

2. Pre-approval checklist

Getting pre-approved
Apply with at least 3 lenders within a 14-45 day window
Compare full Loan Estimates — rate, APR, and itemised fees — not just the headline rate
Choose a lender and obtain your official pre-approval letter
Confirm your pre-approval amount aligns with your personal comfortable budget, not just the maximum approved

3. House hunting checklist

Finding and offering on a home
Work with a buyer's agent who represents your interests specifically
Research the neighbourhood — schools, commute, future development plans
Make a competitive offer with appropriate contingencies for your risk comfort
Don't skip the home inspection, even in a competitive market
Confirm the appraisal meets or exceeds your offer price

4. Closing day checklist

Final steps before keys in hand
Avoid any new debt or credit activity right up to closing — lenders do a final check
Complete a final walkthrough of the property
Review your Closing Disclosure carefully — compare it against your original Loan Estimate
Confirm wire transfer instructions directly with your title company — wire fraud targets closings specifically
Bring required ID and certified funds for closing costs and down payment
Wire fraud is a real risk at closing

Scammers sometimes intercept email threads near closing and send fake wire instructions. Always confirm wiring details by phone, using a number you've independently verified — never one provided only in an email.

5. Worked example — following the checklist in practice

Worked example
Kevin uses the checklist over a 4-month buying journey
4 months Total timeline
3 lenders Compared
$2,100 Saved via checklist diligence

Kevin worked through the financial prep checklist first, discovering and disputing a $340 collection error on his credit report that was incorrectly listed — removing it improved his score by roughly 25 points before he ever applied.

Following the pre-approval checklist, he applied with three lenders in the same week. The offers ranged meaningfully, and choosing the best one saved him approximately $35/month — roughly $12,600 over the loan's life.

At closing, following the wire fraud warning, Kevin called his title company directly using a number from their official website — not the one in an email he'd received — and discovered the email instructions had indeed been altered by a scammer. This single checklist step likely saved him from losing his entire down payment to fraud.

None of these outcomes were luck — they were the direct result of working through a structured checklist rather than relying on memory or assuming everything would be handled correctly by default.

Frequently asked questions

Ideally 6-12 months before you plan to buy, especially if any credit repair or significant saving is needed. The financial preparation stage benefits most from extra time — most of the house hunting and closing stages happen on a faster, more externally-driven timeline.
