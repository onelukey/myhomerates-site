When Does Refinancing Actually Make Sense?

11 min read
Mike Lucas — Founder

Refinance offers can look compelling on paper — a lower rate, a smaller payment — without revealing the full picture of whether the move actually makes financial sense for your specific situation. This guide focuses specifically on the judgment calls: when to say yes, and when to say no. For the full mechanics of how refinancing works, see our complete refinancing guide.

Who this guide is for

Homeowners who've received a refinance offer, or are wondering whether to actively seek one, and want a clear-eyed framework for the decision.

1. The one calculation that matters most

Break-even point = total closing costs ÷ monthly savings. If you'll stay in the home beyond that point, refinancing saves money. If not, it likely costs more than it saves. This single calculation should be the deciding factor in almost every refinance decision — not the headline rate alone.

Always ask for the exact dollar closing costs, not just a percentage

"2-5% of the loan amount" is a useful planning range, but get the actual itemised number from your Loan Estimate before calculating your specific break-even point.

2. Red flags — when to walk away

You might move within 2-3 years. Most refinances need several years to recoup closing costs through monthly savings.
The new term resets your payoff clock significantly. Refinancing into a new 30-year term after already paying 10 years extends your total debt timeline considerably.
You're being pressured to decide quickly. Legitimate refinance offers don't typically require same-day decisions.
The rate improvement is marginal. A 0.25% reduction rarely justifies thousands in closing costs unless your loan balance is very large.
You're cash-out refinancing for discretionary spending. Using home equity for non-essential spending increases your debt against an appreciating asset — worth careful thought.

3. Good reasons to refinance

  • Your rate is genuinely meaningfully above current market rates and you're staying long-term
  • You want to eliminate PMI or FHA MIP after building sufficient equity
  • You're switching from an ARM to a fixed rate before an unfavourable adjustment
  • You're consolidating higher-interest debt at a meaningfully lower blended rate (with discipline not to re-accumulate that debt)
  • You're funding a genuinely value-adding home improvement that increases the property's worth

4. Costs people forget to count

Beyond the obvious closing costs, factor in:

  • Resetting your amortisation — a new loan resets the interest-heavy early payment period
  • New escrow account setup — sometimes requires additional upfront deposit
  • Appraisal fee — typically required, even if you've refinanced before
  • Time and effort — gathering documentation and going through underwriting again

5. Worked example — an offer that looked good but wasn't

Worked example
Patricia receives an unsolicited refinance offer
7.2% → 6.5% Rate "improvement"
$8,400 Closing costs
~18 months Patricia's planned stay

Patricia receives a mailer offering to refinance her $280,000 balance from 7.2% to 6.5% — a 0.7% improvement that sounds attractive. Her monthly savings would be approximately $130. Closing costs are $8,400.

Break-even: $8,400 ÷ $130 = approximately 65 months (over 5 years).

Patricia's company has indicated a likely relocation in 18 months. At that timeline, she would recoup only about $2,340 of her $8,400 in closing costs before selling — a net loss of roughly $6,060 if she proceeds with this refinance.

Despite the genuinely real rate improvement, this is a clear case where the math says no — illustrating why the rate alone should never be the deciding factor.
